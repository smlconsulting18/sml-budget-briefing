VOL. I · NO. 01 ❦ Client Briefing ❦ Melbourne 2026
Federal Budget · 2026–27

A Reckoning · Tax Reform Unpacked

What the May 2026 Budget Means for Australian Investors
On 12 May 2026, the Federal Government handed down the most ambitious package of tax reform in twenty-six years. Negative gearing, the 50% CGT discount, and discretionary trusts — three pillars of middle-Australia investing for a generation — will be substantially rewritten from 1 July 2027. This briefing distils what changes, when, who is affected, and what to do about it.
The Headline

If you hold an investment property, operate through a family trust, or sit on long-term capital gains, this Budget materially reshapes your next decade. The two dates that matter most are 7:30 PM AEST, 12 May 2026 (when grandfathering of existing assets cut off) and 1 July 2027 (when the new rules switch on).

Part One · Negative Gearing

The Four-Bucket FrameworkHow acquisition date now determines everything

The new rules sort investors into four categories based on when the property was acquired. The treatment of each could not be more different. Bucket I is untouched; Bucket IV is, paradoxically, advantaged; Buckets II and III face a graduated tightening.

The pivotal moment is 7:30 PM AEST on 12 May 2026 — "Budget night". Anything held or under signed contract before that timestamp enjoys full grandfathering.
Bucket I
Existing Holdings
Acquired ≤ 12 May 2026 (7:30 PM)
Fully Grandfathered

All investment properties held or under contract before Budget night. Rules unchanged. Rental losses continue to be deductible against any income. Retain contracts of sale and settlement statements to evidence grandfathering status.

Bucket II
Transitional
13 May 2026 — 30 June 2027
One-Year Window

Until 30 June 2027, losses remain deductible against any income. From 1 July 2027, losses can only offset other residential investment income, with unused amounts carried forward. Cash-flow stress-testing for FY28 onwards is essential.

Bucket III
Established Property
Acquired ≥ 1 July 2027
Significantly Restricted

Second-hand residential property purchased after 1 July 2027: losses may only offset other residential property income, never wages or salary. The classic "buy an inner-suburbs renovator and gear it against your salary" play is closed.

Bucket IV
New Builds
Acquired ≥ 1 July 2027
Fully Preserved

House-and-land packages, off-the-plan apartments, and knock-down-rebuilds that add dwellings. Negative gearing fully retained. Critically, the "new build" status transfers only once — the next investor purchaser inherits established-property treatment.

Two Roads for Chen, the Specialist Surgeon

Chen earns $200,000 (top marginal rate, 47%). In September 2027 he is weighing a residential investment expected to produce a $35,000 annual loss.

Option A — established property (Bucket III): the $35,000 loss cannot offset his salary. Refund in year one: nil; the loss carries forward indefinitely.
Option B — off-the-plan (Bucket IV): $35,000 × 47% = $16,450 refund, every year.

Over a ten-year hold, the negative-gearing advantage alone is worth $164,500 — before any capital-gains differential.

Part Two · Capital Gains Tax

The End of the 50% DiscountInflation indexation returns, with a floor

The 50% CGT discount — a fixture of Australian tax since 1999 — is repealed for individuals, trusts, and partnerships alike. In its place: cost-base indexation (only the real gain above inflation is taxable) coupled with a 30% minimum tax rate. The effect is sharply distributional. Long-held, slow-growing assets in inflationary periods may pay less; short-held, fast-growing assets pay materially more.

A Tale of Two Sales

Ten-year hold · Nominal gain $800,000 · Salary $200,000

RegimeTaxable GainTax Payable
Old (50% discount)$400,000$188,000
New (indexation + 30% floor)$500,000 real$240,000
Additional tax$52,000

Modest Winners

Assets held for fifteen-to-twenty years through periods of meaningful inflation. The indexed cost base can lift well above the original purchase price, materially shrinking the real gain. The 30% floor still applies, but the win can be real.

The Clear Losers

Assets that have grown faster than inflation — the typical Melbourne or Sydney capital-city property of the last decade. Real gain ≈ nominal gain. The 50% shield is gone; the 30% floor caps it on the downside but not the upside.

The Election

New-Build Holders May Choose

Investors who acquire a new build after 1 July 2027 can elect, at sale, between the old 50% discount and the new indexation regime — whichever is more favourable. This sits on top of the negative-gearing preservation, creating a meaningful two-fold advantage for new construction.

The dividing line: reform applies only to gains accruing after 1 July 2027. Gains accumulated before that date retain the 50% discount. The practical implication: most existing assets need a 1 July 2027 market valuation to draw the line cleanly. See Part Six.

Part Three · Discretionary Trusts

30% Minimum Tax & the Exit WindowThe end of income splitting as we know it

From 1 July 2028, distributions from discretionary trusts will be subject to a minimum 30% tax rate. The bedrock strategy of streaming income to lower-rate family members — adult children at university, a non-working spouse, retired parents — is, in most cases, neutralised.

Two Important Exemptions

Income-Support Recipients

Distributions to beneficiaries on Age Pension, Disability Support Pension, or comparable income support are exempt from the 30% floor and remain taxable at the recipient's marginal rate. Families with pensioned parents retain partial splitting benefits — though Centrelink income tests must be considered.

Small Business CGT Concessions

The Division 152 concessions — 15-year exemption, 50% active-asset reduction, retirement exemption, rollover — are fully preserved. Owner-operators approaching retirement retain access to a near-complete CGT exemption on the sale of their business.

The Walker Family Trust

Annual trust income of $300,000. Current distributions: husband $80k (alongside $200k salary), wife $80k (homemaker), university-aged son $40k, pensioned mother $30k, undistributed $70k.

FY28 under old rules: approximately $50,000 total tax.
FY29 under new rules (no restructure): approximately $18,000 of additional tax per year, indefinitely.

The Golden Window

1 July 2027 — 30 June 2030

A three-year window of CGT and income-tax rollover relief permits restructuring from a discretionary trust into a Pty Ltd company structure with no immediate tax event.

Inside the new corporate vehicle, profits are taxed at 25% (base rate entity) and distributed as fully franked dividends. Lower-income family shareholders, through franking credits, can achieve effective tax rates below 30% — in some cases producing a cash refund from the ATO.

The warning: miss this window and a future restructure crystallises every dollar of accumulated unrealised gain at once. For a mid-sized family trust, the immediate CGT bill could easily exceed $200,000.

Earlier is not always better.
Whether, when, and how to restructure
is a three-way trade-off — assets, family, retirement horizon.

Part Four · Personal Income Tax

Cuts & the Instant DeductionModest relief for the wage earner

Against the three big repeals above, the personal income tax side of the Budget is unambiguously a giveaway. Four measures matter.

The Hidden Value in the $1,000 Instant Deduction

From FY27, employees and sole traders may claim up to $1,000 of work-related expenses without retaining receipts. The catch: it is an alternative to, not an addition to, the traditional substantiated deduction.

Who Actually Benefits

Sarah, registered nurse. Historical work-expense claims of $200 per year. Under the new regime she automatically receives the $1,000 deduction: additional $800 × 32.5% marginal rate = $260 refund, zero administrative burden.

James, IT consultant. Substantiated claims of $3,500 per year (home office, professional development, devices). Continues claiming with substantiation. The $1,000 instant deduction is irrelevant; no additional benefit.

Part Five · Small Business

Permanent IAWO & Loss Carry-BackTwo measures with real cash-flow impact

$20,000 Instant Asset Write-Off — Made Permanent

Businesses with turnover under $10 million can permanently and immediately write off depreciable assets costing less than $20,000 each. The annual cliffhanger over whether the measure will be extended is finally over.

Espresso Capital Expenditure

A café with $800k annual turnover purchases an $18,000 commercial espresso machine in August 2026. The full $18,000 is deductible in year one. At the 25% base-rate company tax, the cash saving is $4,500 in the year of acquisition — versus depreciation over six-to-eight years under the standard regime.

Loss Carry-Back — Cash Out of Past Tax Paid

Companies with turnover up to $1 billion may apply current-year losses against tax paid in the preceding two income years, generating a cash refund. Approximately 85,000 companies — overwhelmingly SMEs — directly benefit.

Cash Flow When It Matters

A retail company earned $200,000 and paid $50,000 in tax in FY25. In FY27, conditions deteriorate and it posts a $150,000 loss.

Old rule: the loss is carried forward, deductible only when (and if) future profits return.
New rule: $150,000 carried back against FY25 tax → immediate $37,500 cash refund from the ATO.

Part Six · The Valuation Imperative

Why 1 July 2027 Demands a ValuationDrawing the line between old and new gain

Because the CGT reform applies only to gains accruing after 1 July 2027, every existing asset enters a hybrid period — pre-cut-off gain retains the 50% discount, post-cut-off gain runs under the new indexation regime. To split the gain cleanly when the asset is eventually sold, the market value at 1 July 2027 must be established with evidence.

A Word on Quality

What the ATO Will Not Accept

Agent appraisals, bank valuations, and self-prepared estimates are not acceptable for CGT purposes. The ATO requires an independent valuer with CPV, API, or RICS credentials, and the report must explicitly state it is prepared "for CGT purposes".

The valuation fee itself is added to the cost base, providing partial recovery against eventual CGT.
